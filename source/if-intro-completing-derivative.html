    A Perfect Derivative.

    Now that you have seen how to combine two terms into a single derivative by reversing the product rule, you are ready to see how this could help you solve a differential equation. Suppose you are given the following equation
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation} \ub{e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y}_{\text{expanded product rule?}} = 5e^{2x}.\tag{8} \end{equation}
    Seeing the two terms with a derivative suggests that it may be possible to reverse a product rule. In fact, the left side of (8) is a â€œperfect derivativeâ€ since it already has all the pieces needed to reverse a product rule, as shown below:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx} [ e^{2x} y ] = 5e^{2x}.\tag{9} \end{equation}
    This transformation is powerful because it allows us to solve the equation through direct integration, as illustrated in the next example.
    ğŸ”—

    as a single derivative, then find the general solution by direct integration.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    We rewrite the left side as a single derivative by identifying it as a product rule that can be reversed. In the second term, we set \(g\) to \(y\text{,}\) which automatically determines what \(g'\) and \(f\) must be in the first term:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \underset{\Large f}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{e^{x^4}}}}\ \underset{\Large g'}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}\frac{dy}{dx}}}} + \underset{\Large g}{ \underset{\uparrow}{\ul{\vphantom{|}y}}}\ (4x^3 e^{x^4}) \end{equation*}
    and since \(\ f' = [e^{x^4}]' = 4x^3 e^{x^4}\ \text{,}\) the left side is a â€œperfect derivativeâ€ since it has everything it needs to reverse a product rule and bundle the terms into a single derivative, like so
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} e^{x^4} \frac{dy}{dx} + 4x^3e^{x^4} y = \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{x^4} y\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
